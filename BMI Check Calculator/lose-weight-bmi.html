How to Lose Weight Based on Your BMI Result

📅 Updated March 2025 ⏱️ 7 min read ✅ Medically reviewed

Your BMI isn't just a number — it's a starting point for a realistic, personalised weight management plan. The right approach to losing weight looks very different depending on whether your BMI is 26 or 36. This guide breaks down science-backed strategies for each BMI category.

Step 1: Find Your Starting Point

Before setting any weight loss goal, you need three numbers: your current BMI, your Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE), and your target BMI (or target weight).

BMI 25–27.5: Slightly Overweight

At this range, small, sustainable changes make the biggest difference. Aggressive restriction is unnecessary and often counterproductive.

Recommended approach:

  • Create a modest calorie deficit of 200–300 kcal/day — equivalent to cutting one sugary drink and taking a 20-minute walk daily
  • Focus on replacing ultra-processed foods with whole foods rather than counting calories obsessively
  • Add 2–3 strength training sessions per week to preserve muscle while losing fat
  • Target loss rate: 0.25–0.5 kg per week

Realistic timeline: To reach a BMI of 23 from a BMI of 26 (approximately 5–8 kg), expect 12–20 weeks at this pace.

BMI 27.5–30: Overweight

At this range, health risks begin to increase noticeably — blood pressure, blood glucose, and cholesterol often start trending upward. Weight loss has documented health benefits at this stage.

Recommended approach:

  • Calorie deficit of 400–500 kcal/day
  • Aim for 150+ minutes of moderate aerobic exercise per week (brisk walking, cycling, swimming)
  • Prioritise protein (1.6–2.0 g/kg body weight) to preserve muscle during weight loss
  • Target loss rate: 0.5 kg per week
💡 Research shows that losing just 5% of body weight significantly reduces blood pressure, improves blood sugar control, and reduces inflammation markers — even before reaching a "normal" BMI.

BMI 30–35: Obese Class I

At this range, structured intervention delivers the most benefit. Self-directed diet change should be combined with professional support where possible.

Recommended approach:

  • Calorie deficit of 500–750 kcal/day
  • Consider working with a registered dietitian for personalised meal planning
  • Start with low-impact exercise (walking, swimming, cycling) to protect joints
  • Track food intake using an app — research consistently shows tracking improves outcomes
  • Target loss rate: 0.5–0.75 kg per week

BMI ≥ 35: Obese Class II/III

At BMI ≥ 35, medically supervised weight management is recommended. This doesn't mean surgery is required — it means having a doctor monitor progress, assess comorbidities, and provide accountability.

Recommended approach:

  • Consult your GP or an obesity specialist before starting any program
  • Structured very-low-calorie approaches (800–1200 kcal/day) may be appropriate but require medical supervision
  • Medications (GLP-1 agonists such as semaglutide/Ozempic, orlistat) may be appropriate — discuss with a doctor
  • Bariatric surgery produces the most durable results for BMI ≥ 40 or BMI ≥ 35 with comorbidities

The Science of Sustainable Weight Loss

Regardless of BMI category, the same physiological principles apply:

PrincipleWhy It Matters
Calorie deficit (500 kcal/day = ~0.5 kg/week)Physics of weight loss — 7,700 kcal = 1 kg fat
High protein intake (1.6–2.0 g/kg)Preserves muscle, increases satiety, higher thermic effect
Resistance training 2–3×/weekMaintains metabolic rate, improves body composition
7–9 hours sleepSleep deprivation increases hunger hormones (ghrelin) and cravings
Stress managementChronic stress elevates cortisol, promoting fat storage and carb cravings

Why Crash Diets Fail

Severely restricting calories (below 1,200 for women or 1,500 for men) triggers adaptive thermogenesis — your body reduces its metabolic rate to compensate. Muscle is broken down for energy. When normal eating resumes, the lowered metabolism causes rapid fat regain, often overshooting original weight. This cycle, called "yo-yo dieting," is associated with worse long-term metabolic outcomes than no dieting at all.

The research is clear: slow, steady loss of 0.5–1.0 kg per week, with protein-rich eating and resistance training, produces better body composition and is far more sustainable over 12+ months.
