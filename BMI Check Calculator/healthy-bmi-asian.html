What Is a Healthy BMI for Asian Adults?

📅 Updated March 2025 ⏱️ 5 min read ✅ Medically reviewed

If you're South Asian, East Asian, or Southeast Asian, the standard BMI classification you see on most calculators may not accurately reflect your health risks. Research over the past two decades has consistently shown that Asian adults develop type 2 diabetes, hypertension, and cardiovascular disease at lower BMI values than White European adults.

The Standard WHO BMI Ranges

The World Health Organization's standard cut-offs were developed primarily from studies of European populations:

BMI RangeWHO Category
< 18.5Underweight
18.5 – 24.9Normal weight
25.0 – 29.9Overweight
≥ 30.0Obese

Why Asian Adults Need Lower Thresholds

Asian adults tend to have a higher percentage of body fat — especially visceral (abdominal) fat — at the same BMI compared to White European adults. Visceral fat is metabolically active and directly associated with insulin resistance, elevated blood glucose, and cardiovascular inflammation.

🔬 A landmark meta-analysis published in The Lancet found that South Asians had a 2–3 times greater risk of diabetes at a given BMI compared to European populations. At a BMI of 25, an Indian adult has approximately the same diabetes risk as a White European adult at a BMI of 30.

Asian-Specific BMI Thresholds

Based on extensive research, the WHO Expert Consultation on BMI in Asian Populations (2004) recommended lower cut-off points for Asian adults:

BMI RangeAsian CategoryStandard WHO Category
< 18.5UnderweightUnderweight
18.5 – 22.9Normal weightNormal weight
23.0 – 24.9Overweight (At risk)Still "Normal"
25.0 – 29.9Obese I (Moderate risk)Overweight
≥ 30.0Obese II (High risk)Obese

Which Asian Populations Are Affected?

The lower thresholds apply broadly across Asian populations, though exact cut-offs vary between studies:

  • South Asian (India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, Nepal): Strongest evidence for lower thresholds. Indian guidelines use BMI ≥ 23 for overweight.
  • East Asian (China, Japan, Korea): Japan uses BMI ≥ 25 as the obesity threshold; Chinese health authorities recommend ≥ 24 for overweight.
  • Southeast Asian (Indonesia, Malaysia, Philippines, Thailand, Vietnam): Most health authorities recommend using ≥ 23 for overweight.

Waist Circumference: A Better Measure for Asians

Because BMI doesn't account for fat distribution, waist circumference is particularly important for Asian adults. Abdominal fat carries higher metabolic risk than fat stored elsewhere.

Asian waist circumference thresholds (WHO 2008):

  • Men: ≥ 90 cm (35.4 inches) = elevated risk
  • Women: ≥ 80 cm (31.5 inches) = elevated risk

These are lower than the thresholds used for European populations (men: ≥ 102 cm; women: ≥ 88 cm).

Practical Implications

If you're of Asian heritage and your BMI falls between 23–25, you should be aware that your metabolic risk may be higher than the "normal" classification suggests. This doesn't mean you need to lose weight urgently — but it does mean regular screening for blood pressure, blood glucose, and lipid levels is especially important.
