How Many Hours of Sleep Do You Need?

Sleep needs vary by age, lifestyle, and individual biology. The National Sleep Foundation and CDC provide these evidence-based guidelines:

👶 Newborns (0–3 months)

14–17 hours per day

🧒 School Age (6–13)

9–11 hours per day

🧑 Teenagers (14–17)

8–10 hours per day

🧑‍💼 Adults (18–64)

7–9 hours per day

👴 Seniors (65+)

7–8 hours per day

🤰 Pregnant Women

8–10 hours (+ daytime naps)

Why 90-Minute Sleep Cycles Matter

A full sleep cycle lasts approximately 90 minutes and consists of 4–5 stages including light sleep, deep sleep (slow-wave), and REM (Rapid Eye Movement) sleep. Your body cycles through these stages 4–6 times per night.

The key insight: waking up mid-cycle (during deep sleep) causes sleep inertia — that groggy, disoriented feeling that can last for hours. Waking up between cycles (during light sleep) feels effortless by comparison.

⭐ The Golden Rule: Plan your sleep in 90-minute blocks: 4.5h, 6h, 7.5h or 9h. These correspond to 3, 4, 5 or 6 complete cycles. Most adults function best on 5 full cycles (7.5 hours).

The 4 Stages of Sleep

Stage 1 — Light Sleep

1–7 min. Transition from wakefulness. Easy to wake from. Hypnic jerks are common.

Stage 2 — Core Sleep

10–25 min. Heart rate slows, temperature drops. Accounts for ~50% of total sleep.

Stage 3 — Deep Sleep

20–40 min. Hardest to wake from. Critical for physical repair, immune function, memory.

Stage 4 — REM Sleep

10–60 min (lengthens through night). Dreaming occurs. Essential for emotional processing and learning.

❓ FAQ

For most adults, 6 hours is insufficient. Research consistently shows that adults functioning on 6 hours or less show cognitive impairments comparable to those who haven't slept for 24 hours — but often don't perceive themselves as impaired. 7–9 hours is the recommended range for adults. Some people are genuine "short sleepers" due to a rare genetic variant (DEC2 mutation), but this affects less than 3% of the population.

Weekend catch-up sleep can help reduce some metabolic consequences of sleep debt, but it doesn't fully reverse the cognitive or immune impairments accumulated during the week. Social jetlag (shifting sleep times between weekdays and weekends) is also associated with increased obesity and metabolic risk. Consistent sleep timing 7 days a week is far superior to catch-up sleep.
Sleep hygiene refers to habits that promote consistent, quality sleep. Key practices include: keeping a consistent bedtime/wake time (even on weekends), keeping your bedroom dark, cool (65–68°F / 18–20°C), and quiet, avoiding screens 1 hour before bed (blue light suppresses melatonin), avoiding caffeine after 2pm, and avoiding large meals within 3 hours of bedtime. Physical exercise improves sleep quality significantly — but avoid intense exercise within 2 hours of bedtime.
